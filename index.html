Welcome to the mobile web

At the Starbucks near Columbia University, dozens of students sought refuge from one of New York City's worst snow storms in 2018 over their cups of hot coffee. At a nearby table, a hoodie-wearing web developer sat intently staring at lines of colorful code as he began a new project. He had been planning to attend Google's AMP Roadshow, a day-long workshop intended to promote the company's answer to the slow load times of content-heavy webpages on mobile devices. Unfortunately for Veekas Shrivastava, even the technology industry's most powerful company could not justify opening its Manhattan office for visitors that day, forcing the developer to relocate to his neighborhood coffee shop to work through the tutorials on his own.

He began the first workshop entitled "Create Your First AMP Page", in which he learned the minimum standards of an AMP-compliant page. Many of these standards meant to promote responsive design would be familiar to any web developer in 2018, but there are some additions. Shrivastava also found it interesting that Google decided to forego old-fashioned HTML <img> tags for <amp-img>, and the same 'amp-' prefix for <audio> and <video>. He read that they did this to control the layout and prioritize resource-loading. Although the tutorial described how to link non-AMP and AMP pages by linking them in the <head> tag, he found it difficult to imagine a scenario in which a developer would want to duplicate code instead of providing fallback tags. He read about some convenient in-line image layout tricks such as 'sizes="50vw"' and 'layout="responsive"'.
